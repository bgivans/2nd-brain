# AI in Education — Rubric Flip

> February 8, 2026. A session on how colleges are (and aren't) adapting to AI. Connects directly to existing thinking in [[Beliefs/Schooling Rubrics are out dated]] and [[Ideas/Rubric Flip Framework]].

---

## The Core Observation

The conversation explored college AI adaptation — specifically: schools are banning AI or building honor code walls around it, while the real world is building AI into every professional workflow.

The problem this creates: **students are being trained to perform tasks that AI will handle for them within 2 years of graduation.** The rubric being graded on is the wrong rubric.

This is the same argument in [[Beliefs/Schooling Rubrics are out dated]] — schools are measuring compliance with outdated benchmarks rather than measuring what makes someone valuable in the real world.

---

## The New Rubric

What actually matters — and what schools should be teaching and grading:

| Old Rubric | New Rubric |
|-----------|-----------|
| Can you write a 5-paragraph essay? | Can you direct AI to produce something excellent? |
| Can you memorize and recall? | Can you synthesize, evaluate, and apply? |
| Can you follow the format? | Can you identify what the format is optimizing for? |
| Can you work alone? | Can you collaborate with tools and people? |
| Can you produce output? | Can you verify, refine, and own output? |

The new rubric is not "AI does your homework." It's "AI is a collaborator — can you lead it?"

---

## The Business Angle

This connects to GG's positioning: businesses that want to use AI but don't know how to direct it need someone who understands both the output and the workflow. That's the operator's value.

The college angle: if students graduate without AI collaboration skills, they enter a workforce that expects those skills — and they're behind before they start. There's a product or program here for someone willing to build it.

---

## Connection to Rubric Flip Framework

The [[Ideas/Rubric Flip Framework]] is the business version of this argument — organizations running on outdated rubrics (measuring the wrong things, rewarding the wrong behaviors) are quietly dying. The AI adaptation failure in education is the same rubric problem applied to institutions.

---

## Status

Not a business Bryce is building right now. But the thinking is directly applicable to GG's AI positioning — specifically in conversations with clients who are nervous about AI or whose teams are resistant to adoption.

The reframe for those conversations: *"The question isn't whether AI replaces your team. It's whether you want your team leading AI or being replaced by the people who do."*
