# Beyond Typing: Fixing Academic Writing for the AI Era

By Bryce Givans

Givansglobal.com

## AI didn’t kill learning. It exposed outdated rubrics, broken incentives, and low-level thinking.

### Reality check (the classroom already changed)

In 2025, student AI use stopped being “emerging” and became normal.Studies across 16 countries 86% of students report regularly using AI in their studies, Harvard undergraduate survey found nearly 90% using generative AI.

So this is not a future policy problem. It is a current measurement problem.

And the reason this matters is simple:

If your rubric rewards output compliance, students will optimize output compliance. AI just makes that faster.

I’m not a professor. I’m not an academic researcher. I’m a systems thinker and creative technologist who builds workflows that turn messy information into clear decisions. I work with people who do not get rewarded for sounding academic. They get rewarded for being right, being clear, and moving fast.

That is why academic writing, as it is currently taught, looks like it has a hidden bottleneck.

---

## The villain: compliance-first rubrics

Here’s the uncomfortable truth:

Academic writing didn’t get weaker because students got dumber. It got weaker because we taught the wrong target.

A lot of schooling still treats sentence polish as the main event. Grammar. formatting. word count. “academic voice.”

Those matter, but they are not the bottleneck anymore.

The bottleneck is research competence:

- choosing a question worth answering  
      
    
- finding credible sources fast  
      
    
- separating signal from noise  
      
    
- building a defensible argument  
      
    
- verifying key claims  
      
    
- communicating with clarity, not bulk  
      
    

When those skills are weak, “better writing” just becomes better-looking fluff.

And fluff is exactly what compliance rubrics produce.

---

## What we reward is what we get

Most students are not lazy. They are rational.

If your grading system rewards:

- longer papers  
      
    
- rigid templates  
      
    
- citation quantity over source quality  
      
    
- formatting perfection  
      
    
- sounding “academic”  
      
    

you will get students who optimize for those things.

AI did not create this problem. It revealed it.

Because if the assignment is basically “produce correct-looking text,” then yes, a tool that produces correct-looking text will feel like cheating.

The fix is not “ban the tool.”

The fix is “stop measuring the wrong thing.”

---

## The Rubric Flip (before vs after)

If you want academic writing to matter in the AI era, you need a rubric that rewards thinking, not typing.

|   |   |
|---|---|
|What many rubrics reward (old world)|What the AI-era rubric must reward (new world)|
|Word count and length|Clarity constraints (say it in fewer words)|
|Formatting compliance|Reasoning transparency (show how you got there)|
|“Academic voice”|Question quality (is it worth answering?)|
|Citation quantity|Source quality + correct use of evidence|
|Smooth paragraphs|Synthesis (not summary)|
|Grammar as a proxy for rigor|Verification rigor (prove key claims)|
|Template-following|Counterargument strength|
|Final paper only|Proof-of-work artifacts (process + validation)|

If a school wants to keep grading writing, fine. But grade the parts that still matter when AI can draft.

## Stop arguing “AI good vs AI bad.” Argue “what produces learning.”

The AI debate gets trapped in two extremes:

- “AI is ruining education.”  
      
    
- “AI will save education.”  
      
    

Both are lazy takes.

The real question is: Under what conditions does AI support learning versus replace it?

OECD’s 2026 outlook focuses on effective uses of generative AI and emphasizes that these tools can support learning when guided by clear teaching principles and intentional design.

That framing matters because it supports a higher standard:

- AI is allowed  
      
    
- AI is disclosed  
      
    
- AI is supervised  
      
    
- AI does not replace verification, judgment, and synthesis  
      
    

In other words: we raise the bar instead of lowering it.

## The fix: teach the research stack, not just the essay

This is the model I believe schools should teach explicitly.

### 1) Question formation

Students should be trained to produce questions that survive stress:

- What are you really trying to prove?  
      
    
- What evidence would settle this?  
      
    
- What would change your mind?  
      
    
- Why is this worth answering?  
      
    

### 2) Literature discovery + source triage

Teach students how to:

- prioritize primary sources when possible  
      
    
- distinguish research from opinion  
      
    
- rank credibility  
      
    
- detect recycled takes and citation laundering  
      
    

### 3) Argument mapping

Before students write paragraphs, they should map:

- claim  
      
    
- evidence  
      
    
- warrant (why the evidence supports the claim)  
      
    
- counterargument  
      
    
- response  
      
    
- confidence level  
      
    

### 4) Verification

This is where academic writing should become harder, not easier.

Verification is a skill. It should be graded.

### 5) Writing for clarity

Polish is last, not first.

AI can help here, but it should never substitute for thinking.

---

## Proof-of-work (integrity without surveillance)

If institutions want integrity without turning professors into cops, require artifacts that show thinking:

- Source matrix: claim → sources → strength rating  
      
    
- Assumption log: what you assumed and why  
      
    
- Verification notes: how you validated key claims  
      
    
- A short list of “keystone prompts” (not every prompt, the ones that shaped the direction)  
      
    
- Tool disclosure: what tools were used and how  
      
    

This shifts the culture from hiding to owning.

## My workflow (what “AI as leverage” actually looks like)

When I consult with leaders, I don’t win by writing prettier sentences. I win by building clarity fast.

A simplified version of my workflow:

- Record and transcribe conversations (with permission) using Otter.ai  
      
    
- Capture high-signal notes in Milanote  
      
    
- Use ChatGPT to generate sharper follow-up questions when I hit a ceiling  
      
    
- Use Perplexity for deep research runs and cross-checking  
      
    
- I load the sources into NotebookLM to generate visuals and an audio brief.  
      
    
- Rewrite the plan to a simpler reading level so busy people can absorb it fast and act  
      
    

That is not AI doing the work. That is tools accelerating the parts that are not the bottleneck, so I can spend more time on the parts that are.

---

## Tight close: the challenge

If colleges want to stay relevant, they need to do one hard thing:

Stop grading output compliance as a proxy for thinking.

AI exposed the gap. Now schools choose:

- evolve assessment and teach research-stack literacy  
      
    
- or keep policing formatting while students learn the real skills somewhere else
