# Faith — Core Convictions

> Synthesized from 9 years of church notes, prayer journals, and personal reflections (2017–2026). These are not one-time thoughts — they're ideas that kept returning, rephrasing themselves, and deepening over time. The repetition is the signal.

---

## On Obedience

> *"Obedience is doing something you don't want to do — when you choose to listen to his commandments even when you don't understand them. Obedience with the Lord will turn into a partnership."* — 2017

Discipleship is obedience becoming partnership. Not obligation. Not performance. The relationship changes when you stop asking "why" and start trusting the one who knows better.

God is not in a hurry. He will call when you're ready. Dare you question the all-knowing.

> *"God will not waste what you are faithful in."* — 2019

---

## On Identity

The clearest recurring theme across all years: **identity rooted in being chosen, not in performance.**

- 2017: *"God did not choose you because you are special — you are special because God chose you through all your imperfections."*
- 2021: **Imago Dei** — humans carry the image of God. Not symbolically. Actually. The goal is to more accurately reflect that image.
- 2023: *"I am valuable because I am a child of God. He chose me."*
- 2024: John restates 7 times that he is "the disciple Jesus loved." This was his identity. Not his role, not his performance — just that he was loved.

The inverse: **what is failure?** Looking to the world and believing what it says about you.

## On Fulfillment

A conviction that runs from 2017 through 2023 without contradiction:

> *"If I'm not fulfilled I'm not going to do it. Fulfillment comes from God only."* — 2017

> *"I want to make my life work for me. Make it perfectly set up so I feel whole. Managing your circumstances expecting that one day off will fill you again — and that is never the case."* — 2021

The 2021 breakthrough: fulfillment doesn't come from optimizing your circumstances. It comes from asking where God needs you to serve and showing up there. The tank fills from giving, not from managing how much you have left.

> *"God said: where do you need me and how can I serve. Paul expended his entire life and was given everything."* — 2021

## On Secret Character vs. Stage Character

> *"God is far more concerned with who you are in secret than who you are on stage."* — 2019 (1 Samuel 16)

> *"Character is who you are when no one is around."* — 2017 (Acts 6 / Steven)

> *"Do you work to receive praise or to give praise?"* — 2017

> *"Heart intent for our actions: Am I speaking about God to make me feel better about myself or to bring glory to God?"* — 2024

This one connects directly to the fame trap observation in the music world (2023 journal) and to the Operator identity more broadly — building real things quietly rather than optimizing for how it looks. → [[Context/Operator Profile]]

Steven (Acts 6) is the clearest model for this: filled with the Holy Spirit, theologically deep, courageous and humble — and when lies were spoken against him, he prayed for his enemies as they stoned him. His character was the only thing he had to stand on. He chose not to defend himself.

## On Living in Margin

> *"Temptation often comes disguised as opportunity."* — 2017

> *"If you are always adjusting yourself to accommodate other people you live without margin."* — 2017

> *"Concentrate to eliminate. To be in use in context and in the power of saying no."* — 2017

> *"Margin is running it through the grid to make sure you stick to your priorities."* — 2017

Knowing your mission is what gives you the ability to say no. Without a clear calling, everything looks like an opportunity and you can't tell the difference between the toast being on fire and the kitchen being on fire.

## On People and Relationships

> *"You are who your closest 5 friends are. Who are your 5? Are you trying to help people in need before helping yourself? Balance who you surround yourself with."* — 2017

> *"Walk towards me — not because of how cool I am, but in spite of all my flaws."* — 2019

> *"Stop pursuing people for what you gain from them. Instead ask: what do people gain from knowing me?"* — 2025

> *"Why are people better because I am their friend, son, boyfriend?"* — 2025

The movement from 2017 to 2025 on this: 2017 is about who you surround yourself with. 2025 is about what you offer to others. Same root conviction, different direction.

The Good Samaritan framework (Luke 10, 2025): Jesus made the radically despised person the hero. The religiously correct people walked by. The question is not "who is my neighbor?" — it's "am I being one?"

**Favoritism is a contradiction of the gospel** — the gospel declares all people equally needy and equally loved. The moment you pursue someone for what they can give you, you've left the gospel behind.

## On Suffering and Redemptive Perspective

> *"We also glory in our suffering, because we know that suffering produces perseverance; perseverance, character; and character, hope. And hope does not disappoint us."* — Romans 5:3-5

> *"It is possible to move past the suffering but life ain't over. God is saying: stop looking back and look forward."* — 2017

> *"Hitting walls is a guaranteed thing to happen. Thankfully God will be with you in the moment."* — 2023

**Redemptive perspective** (2025 framing):
- *"What is God shaping in me?"*
- *"How is this building endurance?"*
- *"What is this preparing me for?"*

Pure joy is not happiness. Pure joy is a supernatural delight in the person, purpose, and people of God — even in trials.

## On God and Money

Directly from 2026 church notes, worth capturing now that finances are a central concern:

The Bible talks about money more than faith, grace, love, heaven, or hell — over 2,000 references. Being wealthy is not condemned (Abraham, David, Solomon, Joseph were all wealthy). The problem is what wealth does to the heart.

> *"Entering the kingdom of God while wealthy is a difficult task."* — Matthew 19

**The solution is not poverty. It's surrendered generosity.**

When something has the potential to own your heart — sacrificially give it to God and put him on the throne of that area first.

> *"Stop tipping God."*

Proverbs 13: A good man leaves an inheritance. Proverbs 3: Honor the Lord with your wealth. The goal isn't to avoid money — it's to hold it correctly.

## The Elijah Pattern

Elijah repeatedly tells God he is all alone and there is no hope. God responds: you still have purpose.

> *"God is not in the trembling, not in the fire, not in the wind — but in the whispers. For God is intimate and close with you. We don't whisper to strangers."* — 2017

The pattern appeared in 2017 and echoes in the October 2023 journal entry — feeling purposeless, unclear, surrounded by noise and not knowing what he truly wanted. The whisper is there. The question is whether you're listening.

> *"I currently talk to God but do not listen. I should be having a two-way conversation."* — 2024

## On Repentance

From a June 2025 session exploring what repentance actually means:

Repentance is not the same as guilt. Guilt is about the act. Repentance is about the turn — literally "metanoia" in Greek means *change of mind*, a reorientation of direction.

The question being wrestled with: *what does turning to God actually look like in practice, not just theologically?*

The practical answer:
1. Name the thing specifically — not "I messed up" but *what* specifically
2. Sit with the consequence without rushing to fix it yourself
3. Ask for forgiveness without performing it
4. Change the behavior — not as payment, but as evidence of the turn

The distinction between guilt and repentance matters because guilt can live in the mind indefinitely without producing change. Repentance by definition moves.

## On Betrayal and Anger

From an April 2025 session: Someone did something that felt like betrayal. The session was looking for specific scripture on handling anger and betrayal from a faith perspective.

The relevant framework:

> *"Be angry, and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your anger, and give no opportunity to the devil."* — Ephesians 4:26-27

Anger is not the problem. Anger held without processing it — without giving it to God and being honest about it — creates an opening. The anger itself is information about something that was violated (a trust, an expectation, a relationship).

The Joseph pattern: betrayed by his brothers, sold into slavery, wrongly imprisoned — and positioned by God at the highest point in Egypt specifically *because* of what the betrayal set in motion. Betrayal is not the end of the story. It's often the beginning of the real one.

**Practical note:** Anger in faith contexts is often suppressed because it feels "un-Christian." But the Psalms are full of raw anger expressed directly to God. Praying honestly about anger — not packaging it as "I'm struggling" but stating "I am furious and I need you to be God in this" — is closer to real faith than performance.

## On Burden of Sin in Relationships

From a June 2025 session on sin dynamics in close relationships:

The question: when someone you're in relationship with is actively sinning, how does their sin affect you? Are you responsible for their spiritual state?

The biblical framing:
- You are not responsible for another person's sin — you cannot carry it, fix it, or be condemned by it
- You are responsible for how you respond to it — with truth, with grace, without enabling it
- The weight you feel is empathy (which is appropriate) + the natural consequences of being close to someone who is hurting themselves and others

**The log and speck principle (Matthew 7):** before addressing someone else's sin, check what's operating in you. Not as a way to avoid accountability — but because the approach matters, and approach is shaped by whether you're coming from a clean place.

**On relationships with unbelievers:** 2 Corinthians 6:14 on being "unequally yoked" is often used as a blunt prohibition. The more nuanced reading: what happens to shared vision and direction when the foundation is different? This is worth holding as an honest question, not a formula.

## Open Questions (Still Wrestling)

These haven't been resolved — they're worth sitting with, not closing:

- Why not until Jesus were all people chosen by God?
- Why would the sin of one man pass down through generations?
- If not all relationships with God are the same — is it right to pursue all verses and make efforts to change in all regards? With such diverse minds, can we all become the same "Christian"?
- Why is there an enemy? Is sin simply the product of imperfection and freedom rather than an opposing force?
- Can a perfect thing create an imperfect thing?
- Do we each play a role in the world like pieces on a chess board — if one is removed before God's timing, does the game weaken?
- **Did Jesus write anything himself?** (Feb 2026 inquiry) — The only recorded instance of Jesus writing is in John 8:6-8, where he writes in the sand (contents unknown). Everything else is oral teaching, recorded by the disciples. The question isn't answered — it's noted. What did he write in the dirt? Some theologians believe he was writing the accusers' sins.
